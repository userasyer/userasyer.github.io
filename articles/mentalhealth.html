Indie Media in Representing Depression

 Hi everyone, I am Asyraf and I love modern art. Modern art enables people to creatively express their feelings or shine the spotlight towards issues in society. Now as for the reason I mentioned my affinity towards modern art has to do with media representation of mental health issues.

 I reckon that most of us have watched some form of media representation of mental health issues, be it in bad taste or genuine. Now when talking about media representing mental health issue, especially schizophrenia or DID, I can bet most people would mention movie names, as those are the most popular form of media representation we have in the current century.

 But have you encountered indie media's representation of mental health issue? One I could think of is the song Baby Hotline by Jack Stauber. It is a short song with lyrics that might seem random to first-time listeners, but with further interpretation, one could see the subtle meaning behind all the symbolisms of Stauber's words. The main theme of Baby Hotline is depression and anxiety, among other issues in the current society. One part in the song that says “Baby flatline, still time to do it too” is an eerie yet real representation of somebody struggling with depression contemplating their suicidal thoughts.

 For centuries, art has been one of the most powerful ways people tell stories and express their feelings. Art pieces like At Eternity’s Gate by Vincent Van Gogh, Yer Blues by the Beatles and Baby Hotline by Jack Stauber are examples of art being used to ignite discussion among society regarding mental health issues.

 As for depression, while some people might find media representations inaccurate or exaggerated in nature, there are some positive takeaways from the way art represents this mental health problem. As people encounter and appreciate these forms of artistic expression, they would be more likely to then educate themselves regarding the causes, symptoms and treatments of depression. For instance, Norwich City FC’s “You Are Not Alone” video campaign sparked awareness among fans and beyond on men’s mental health struggles. This shows that using art in a way that intrigues people could help society understand more on things often overlooked.

 To conclude, I think media has been one of the driving forces of societal progress especially when it comes to informing the mass regarding issues that are silently plaguing communities around the world. I hope that more artistic expression of mental health issues will be popularised and discussed about to help the public understand and eradicate prejudice towards people struggling with it.
